Section 1

Start with essential phrases and simple grammar concepts

CEFR A1

This section covers the very early A1 level of CEFR, an international standard of language proficiency.

Here’s how someone at this level might communicate1:

Ich komme aus Mexiko‑Stadt. Ich spreche Spanisch.

All CEFR levels

  • A1: Ich komme aus Mexiko‑Stadt. Ich spreche Spanisch.
  • A2: Ich bin in Neuseeland geboren, aber jetzt lebe ich in Osaka. Ich lebe seit fünf Jahren in Osaka.
  • B1: Kairo liegt am Nil, einem bekannten Fluss. Wie viel weißt du über seine Geschichte?
  • B2: 1755 wurde Lissabon von einem Erdbeben und einem Feuer zerstört, aber dann wurde es in einem modernen Baustil wiederaufgebaut.

Grammar concepts

Gender

Every German noun has a gender2: masculine, feminine, or neuter.

This affects a lot of words — for example, which articles3 you use:

gender example
masculine der Hund
feminine die Katze
neuter das Brot

When learning a new noun4, it’s important to learn its gender2 at the same time.

Verbs

German verbs5 change endings based on the subject6. Here’s an example of a regular verb7:​

subject lernen8
ich lerne
du lernst
er / sie9 / es lernt10
wir lernen
ihr lernt
Sie11 / sie12 lernen

Formality

In German, the way to say you depends on formality.​

Use Sie with someone you don't know well, or in a professional setting.

Frau Schmitt, **Sie sind** sehr nett.

Use du when talking to friends, family, or people your own age.

Alex, **du bist** sehr nett.

Word order

In German, the verb comes in second position. Compare these:

Ich **esse** morgens Pizza.
Morgens **esse** ich Pizza.

Notice how other parts of the sentence move around, but the verb esse is still the second element.

  1. I am from Mexico City. I speak Spanish. ↩

  2. Gender is a way to sort all nouns. In German, every noun is either masculine, feminine, or neuter, even when they don't refer to people. ↩↩

  3. Articles show whether you mean a specific noun (like "the cat") or that noun in a general sense (like "a cat"). ↩

  4. A noun is the name of something, such as a thing (“spoon”), an idea (“love”), a place (“Atlanta”), or a person (“Maria”). ↩

  5. A verb expresses an action (“Luis cooks every day”) or a state of being (“I am sick”). ↩

  6. The subject of a sentence is the person or thing performing the action of the verb. For example, "I" in "I threw the ball." ↩

  7. Regular words follow predictable, standard patterns of change. ↩
